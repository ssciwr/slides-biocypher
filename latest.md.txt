- Biology is extremely relational: encoding, interaction, affecting, targeting

# Ontologies

- Help us agree upon the way to describe our domain
- Have specific ways of characterizing relationships consistently
- Relationships preserve the biological context and its provenance

# We all use relationships to inform science - Medical study example
IVF is extremely difficult and can be unpredictable for women, as it can take many rounds, each of which can take months. We understand the genetic basis for part of the issue behind POF(Premature Ovarian Failure). Can we find commonalities betwen the genetic mutations underlying POF and predictive power to estimate IVF success? 

---
# How we use relationships in science
- We already know certain proteins, e.g. FSH, affect the biological function of reproduction(follicle development and ovarian function).
The variation of a nucleotide from a gene variant changes CGA to GGA.
- This will change the amino acids that make up certain proteins. Those proteins, e.g. FSH, affect the biological function, such as the behaviour of follicles in embryos.
- There could be a correlation between that mutation being expressed, and IVF success. 

-- The point is the chain of events; it all cascades from the mutation of a single nucleotide.

---

# The power of that is:
- The fact that POV(Premature Ovarian Failure) is conceptually linked to IVF enables us to explore this research direction, and in this case can be identified as a Research Question manually.
- But in some cases, we have many different partially related concepts. Different Amino Acids, thosuands of Genes, different ways of categorizing disease.
- Knowledge Graphs can help us to identify and filter the *most promising candidates* at scales of relationship transversal we could never achieve ourselves by hand.

# But my data would take a long time to convert into ontology concepts

It can, but we do not need to do everything manually.

- Entity linking maps text or labels to ontology/database IDs
  e.g. “seizure” → HPO: Seizure; “EGFR” → UniProt/HGNC gene/protein ID

- Annotation adds structured meaning to raw data
  Convert text into a graph node, edge, or property. For free text: scispaCy, MetaMap, CLAMP, MedCAT

- Tabular-to-graph tools can help
  OntoWeaver can map tables into semantic knowledge graphs and can be used with BioCypher-style graph construction

- The goal is not perfect automation
  The goal is a reproducible mapping pipeline where uncertain mappings, thresholds, and assumptions are visible

# Challenges with knowledge graphs
Different ways of describing the same ontologies, or different levels of data granularity for knowledge graph content.

Sometimes, the same biological "unit" can be described at slightly different, overlapping scales.

Different datasets doing this makes managing and drawing insights across omics difficult.

---

The goal of BioCypher is to resolve the difficulty of:

(A) understanding the applicability of knowledge graphs for biological research inquiry in your subdomain  
(B) enabling rapid research with knowledge graphs  
(C) supporting the provision of your own data to this analysis  
(D) sharing your data with the wider scientific community to achieve faster overall progress in biology

---

# BioCypher configures stringent regulation
- Rather than having questions later, such as "is seizure a Symptom, Clinical Finding, or PatientFeature in this context?", you decide when integrating data.
- And when you have 10 curious questions one morning, you want predictability in what you should ask.
- Are disease IDs coded IDs or free text? This may not matter, but all must be consistent. This helps data provenance.

# Example 2: BioCypher for historic, as-yet unconnected, integratable work in reanalysis papers
- 1. Say you have 3 different datasets from different experiments focusing on different genes.
- 2. You can use an adapter to bring the information in, and then use ontology data from, e.g., UniProt, to understand what genetics/proteins/pathways occur in common between those different separate datasets.
- 3. You can make conclusions with the proteins between these 3 datasets in a 4th paper.

# Why do you need a "schema" when you have ontologies already?
- The ontology gives you the general biological vocabulary.
- The schema says how your project will use that vocabulary for this specific dataset, question, and analysis.
- Your grouping of data or other analysis
- Should usually change per experiment
